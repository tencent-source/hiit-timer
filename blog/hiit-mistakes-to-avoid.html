7 HIIT Mistakes That Are Killing Your Results

Tips5 min readUpdated January 2025

HIIT is one of the most effective training methods—when done correctly. But many people unknowingly sabotage their results with common mistakes. Here are the 7 biggest errors and exactly how to fix them.

1
Not Going Hard Enough

The Problem

This is the #1 mistake. "High-intensity" means HIGH intensity—80-95% of your maximum heart rate. If you can easily hold a conversation during work intervals, you're not doing HIIT. You're doing moderate-intensity interval training.

The Fix

During work intervals, you should only be able to speak 2-3 words at most. On a scale of 1-10, you should be at 8-9. By the final rounds, you should feel like you can barely continue. That's when you know you're doing it right.

2
Doing HIIT Every Day

The Problem

True HIIT is extremely taxing on your body and nervous system. Daily HIIT leads to overtraining, elevated cortisol, poor recovery, decreased performance, and increased injury risk.

The Fix

Limit HIIT to 2-3 sessions per week with at least 48 hours between sessions. On other days, do lower-intensity activities like walking, swimming, or yoga. Your body needs recovery time to adapt and improve.

3
Skipping the Warm-Up

The Problem

Going from cold to 90% max effort is a recipe for injury. Cold muscles are tight muscles, and tight muscles are injury-prone muscles. Jumping straight into burpees or sprints puts enormous stress on unprepared tissues.

The Fix

Always spend 5-10 minutes warming up with progressive intensity. Start with light movement (walking, arm circles), then gradually increase to moderate movement (jogging, jumping jacks). Your last warm-up minute should approach 70% effort.

4
Sacrificing Form for Speed

The Problem

When fatigue hits, form often goes out the window. Sloppy burpees, knee-caving squats, and rounded-back movements lead to injury and reduce the effectiveness of the exercise.

The Fix

Prioritize form over speed, always. If your form breaks down, slow down or modify the exercise. It's better to do 8 perfect reps than 15 sloppy ones. Consider filming yourself to check form.

5
Making Workouts Too Long

The Problem

If you can do 45 minutes of "HIIT," you're not doing HIIT. True high-intensity work cannot be sustained for long periods. Extended sessions mean you're unconsciously pacing yourself, which defeats the purpose.

The Fix

Keep HIIT sessions to 20-30 minutes total, including warm-up and cool-down. The actual high-intensity portion should be 10-20 minutes. If you have more time, add a separate strength or steady-state cardio session.

6
Not Tracking Intervals Properly

The Problem

Guessing your intervals leads to inconsistent workouts. You might cut work intervals short when tired or extend rest periods without realizing it. This reduces the training stimulus.

The Fix

Use a dedicated interval timer with audio cues. This lets you focus entirely on effort rather than watching the clock. The audio alerts tell you exactly when to push and when to recover.

7
Neglecting Recovery and Sleep

The Problem

HIIT creates significant stress on your body. Without adequate sleep and recovery, you can't adapt to the training. Chronic under-recovery leads to plateaus, fatigue, and eventually burnout.

The Fix

Aim for 7-9 hours of sleep per night. Eat enough protein (0.7-1g per pound of bodyweight). Stay hydrated. Consider active recovery activities on rest days. Listen to your body—if you're consistently exhausted, you need more recovery.

The Bottom Line

HIIT works—but only when done correctly. Fix these seven mistakes, and you'll see dramatically better results from your training. Remember: it's not about doing more HIIT, it's about doing HIIT better.
